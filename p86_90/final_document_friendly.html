If no communication with helicopter, maintain a steady course, into wind. A wind across the deck of 10-20 knots is ideal. Under no circumstances alter course or speed while winching is taking place. During Transfer

E017

If possible, move the patient to a position as close to the hoist area as his condition permits TIME IS IMPORTANT.

E018

1Allow the hoisting apparatus to contact some steel part of the ship before handling it. This will release any static electricity

E019

2- A guide-line may be attached to the apparatus. Keep same clear from obstacles. The handling of this line (uncharged) is without danger;

E020

3- Take care that the patient's hands remain inside the stretcher. Let the helicopter's winch-man know when he can start heaving. It is worth noting that a crew man will usually be lowered to the ship's deck to effect the lifting of the stretcher or casualty. The motion of the hand is Thumb Up

E021

4- Should it be necessary to unhook the hoisting apparatus; keep the hook clear from the ship and signal-By thumb up-when the cleared hook can be hove in. Never fasten the hook on deck and the stretcher is never to be moved without being unhooked

E022

5- As soon as the patient has been placed in the stretcher then signal the pilot to slack off the wire, hook on and heave. Prevent the stretcher from swinging during hoisting. Use the guide-line, if any, for stabilising and steering the stretcher. Take care that the line is free from feet and legs of the ship's crew!

E023

Hi-Line Technique

E024

In very bad weather, it may not be possible to lower the stretcher for a crewman and stop directly on to the deck. In such a case, a rope extension of the hoist line may be lowered to the ship.

E025

This should be handled by a member of the ship's crew and taken in as the helicopter pays out the hoist cable. Coil down the line on deck clear of snags, but do not make it fast. The helicopter will pay out the full scope of wire and descend, probably to one side of the vessel, while the ship's crew continue to take in the slack until the winch hook and strop are on board.

E026

Whilst taking in the slack, remember that the ship and the helicopter will be moving relative to each other-take great care to keep the line outboard of all obstruction.

E027

A member of the helicopter crew may or may not be lowered with strop. The casualty will then be secured in the strop and, when he or other member of the ship's crew signifies that he is ready, the helicopter will climb and take in the wire. Pay out the extension rope, keeping enough weight on it to keep it taut; until the end is reached, then cast the end clear of the ship's side; unless further evacuation of crew is intended when, if possible, the end of the line should be retained on board to make recovery of the stop for the next man easier-but do not make the line fast. When large numbers of casualties are to be lifted, two strops may be offered simultaneously; one casualty is to be placed in each strop in the normal manner
